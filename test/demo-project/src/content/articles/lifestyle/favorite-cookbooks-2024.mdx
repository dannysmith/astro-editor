# My Favorite Cookbooks of 2024

This year I've been on a mission to become a better home cook. I've bought (and actually used) more cookbooks than in the previous five years combined. Here are the five that have genuinely changed how I cook.

## 1. Salt, Fat, Acid, Heat by Samin Nosrat

This isn't just a cookbook—it's a *framework* for understanding cooking. Nosrat breaks down cooking into four elements:

- **Salt** - enhances flavor
- **Fat** - carries flavor
- **Acid** - balances flavor
- **Heat** - determines texture

<Callout type="info">
Once you understand these four elements, you don't need to follow recipes religiously anymore. You can improvise and adjust based on what you have.
</Callout>

## 3. Ottolenghi Simple

I love *complex* flavors, but I don't always have time for complex recipes. This book delivers restaurant-quality results with minimal effort.

- 10 ingredients or fewer

## 5. An Everlasting Meal by Tamar Adler

Technically this is more philosophy than cookbook, but it's transformed how I approach cooking. Adler makes the case for cooking as an ongoing process rather than isolated events:

- Use vegetable trimmings for stock
- Turn leftover grains into new dishes
- Let each meal inform the next

## How I Actually Use Cookbooks

Here's my system:

1. **Read through** like a novel first (yes, really)
2. **Flag recipes** that excite me with sticky notes
3. **Make shopping lists** for the week based on flagged recipes
4. **Actually cook** instead of just collecting cookbooks

The last step is the most important—these books only helped me because I *used* them.

## Final Thoughts

Good cookbooks are investments. They're not just recipe collections—they're teachers, inspiring you to try new techniques and think differently about food.
